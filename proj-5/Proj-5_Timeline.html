Henry David Thoreau's Walden: A Closer Look at Revisions in "Higher Laws"

This timeline will show the various revisions in the "Higher Laws" chapter within Henry David Thoreau's text Walden. The main focus will be on the revisions in the first and seventh paragraph of this chapter.

Why the "Higher Laws" Chapter of Walden

We chose to focus on the "Higher Laws" chapter of Walden because, for the most part, this chapter received very few revisions in the early versions of Walden. Thoreau wrote multiple versions of Walden, labeled A-G, and most of the revisions in this chapter were done in versions E-F. These included revisions to several pre-existing paragraphs and the addition of new paragraphs. In Versions A-D of this section, we see that most of the text stays the same, with slight revisions made in pencil throughout the manuscript - almost as an after thought by Thoreau - only to be omitted from the final version upon publication.

In Versions E-G, the text revcieved substantial revisions and additions, making it a very interesting opening paragraph for the chapter.

September 1847 — September 1852

Version A, Paragraph 7 in "Higher Laws"

Version A is the first manuscript version of Walden. It shows what Thoreau first wrote during his time in a cabin in the woods. What is interesting about this page, besides the fact that it is the first time that Thoreau is writing and grappling with his thoughts during this time, is that it contains a line at the end of the paragraph written in pencil - as previously stated, this seems like an after thought. This line in particular reads "But practically I am only half-converted by my own arguments for I still fish". This chapter begins with Thoreau discussing fishing and the adventure he feels that it holds. One could infer that in this line, Thoreau is confessing that while he has argued throughout the text that nature should be observed, perhaps he is rather hypocritical in the sense that his own argument has not persuaded him to stop fishing. This is the only version in which this line appears in Walden.

1852 — 1853

Version E, Paragraph 1 in "Higher Laws"

Version E is when you see the first significant revisions to this section of Walden. The first paragraph alone recieved a total rewrite. Thoreau changed the beginning of the paragraph, added a new introduction, made additions in pencil and underlined key words in ink. For instance, Thoreau once again makes an addition in pencil that reads "not that I was hungry, but for the wilderness which he represented". This line follows the description of the beaver crossing in front of Thoreau on his walk home. In this line, it is almost as if Thoreau did not want the reader to think of him as some sort of savage, but wanted to provide justification - Thoreau was so immersed in Nature at this point in the text he wanted to be apart of it as much as possible. He ends the portion of revisions by noting, "I love the wild not less than the good".

September 1852 — 1853

Version E, Paragraph 7 in "Higher Laws"

In Version E, Thoreau made small word choice revisions specifically to this paragraph. He changed the word "I" in the text to a more general term, such as "one" and "him". We believe that Thoreau made this revision to not only make the text more general and appliciable to everyone, but also to separate his experiences from the text. Additionally, this paragraph used to be the ending of the chapter in previous versions, but at this point in the revisions, Thoreau added many more paragraphs to the manuscript, extending the length of this particular section of text.

1853 — 1854

Version F, Paragraph 1 in "Higher Laws"

In this set of revisions, Thoreau made some changes to word choice and once again made revisions in pencil to the text. Many of these penciled-in revisions work to give the reader a closer look into what Thoreau experienced on his time in the woods. In this set of revisions, specifically those written in pencil, it appears as if Thoreau is trying to convey a sense of savagery and insanity that occured during this time. In the revised line, added in pencil, Thoreau writes, "Once or Twice, however, while I lived at the pond, I found myself ranging the woods, like a half-starved hound, with a strange abandonment, seeking some kind of venison which I might devour, and no morsel could have been too savage for me". This line of text seems to give a unique view on Thoreau's mental health during his time in the woods. Many of the revisions made in this version seem to point to how Thoreau changed while in the woods and how his thinking has been affected because of it.

1853 — 1854

End of Revisions for Paragraph 7 of "Higher Laws"

After the revisions in Version E, the seventh paragraph in "Higher Laws" received no further changes. This was the case for many portions of Thoreau's Walden. Some portions are never revised, some are revised often, and some are revised just a few times.

Conclusion

The "Higher Laws" chapter of Henry David Thoreau's Walden provides an insightful look into Thoreau's time in the woods and his mental health during that time through the many revisions he made to this section of the text. By using pencil, Thoreau shows that many of the revisions he made were an after-thought of his original text, as well as thoughts that can be shocking to read and savage in nature. Other times, the penciled-in text seems to have been meant to clarify his orginial sentence and why his actions were justifiable (think of Thoreau wanting to devour the beaver to become closer with nature) - many of these revisions were later omitted from the final text. All in all, one can learn a lot by looking at the many revisions that Thoreau made to Walden over the years and by looking at the way in which he made those revisions, as shown by the revisions that were made to the chapter.
